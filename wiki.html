The Protection of Children from Sexual Offences (POCSO) Act, 2012 provides comprehensive legal protection for children against sexual abuse, exploitation, and pornography. It mandates stringent actions against perpetrators and ensures child-friendly legal processes. The act recognizes various forms of abuse, including assault, harassment, and use of children in pornography, ensuring that children have the right to be safe from all forms of harm.

The Prohibition of Child Marriage Act, 2006 prohibits the marriage of girls under the age of 18 and boys under the age of 21. This law is designed to prevent child marriages, which are detrimental to the physical and mental health of children, particularly girls. It provides for strict penalties against those who facilitate or participate in child marriages and protects children from being forced into such unions.

The University Grants Commission (UGC) Regulations, 2015, based on the Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition, and Redressal) Act, 2013, ensures the protection of girl students from sexual harassment in educational institutions. Every institution must have an Internal Complaints Committee (ICC) to address complaints related to sexual harassment and ensure a safe and supportive environment for female students

The Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act, 2005 (PWDVA) protects married women from all forms of domestic violence, including physical, emotional, sexual, and economic abuse. The law allows women to seek legal protection, residence orders, and monetary relief, ensuring that they are not forced to stay in abusive relationships. It also provides access to shelter homes, medical aid, and counseling services for women facing domestic violence.

The Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961 makes it illegal to give or receive dowry before, during, or after marriage. This law protects married women from being harassed or abused for dowry by their husbands or in-laws. If dowry demands are made or if a woman is subjected to cruelty over dowry, she can file a complaint under this law, and stringent punishments, including imprisonment and fines, can be imposed on the offenders.

Section 498A of the Indian Penal Code (IPC) provides married women the right to file a complaint against their husband or in-laws for any act of cruelty, whether physical or mental. Cruelty includes harassment for dowry, threats, or inflicting harm on a woman. This section of the law ensures that women can seek legal recourse if subjected to any form of cruelty within their marriage and that the offenders face criminal charges.

Although marital rape is not explicitly criminalized under Indian law, the Supreme Court of India has made significant progress in addressing this issue. In certain cases of sexual violence within marriage, courts have acknowledged the right of women to live with dignity and without sexual harassment. Women can file cases of cruelty under Section 498A or seek divorce on the grounds of sexual abuse.

Married women who work have the right to protection from sexual harassment at their workplace under the Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition, and Redressal) Act, 2013. This law ensures that married women, like any other female employees, can report instances of sexual harassment at work, and employers must address the complaints through an Internal Complaints Committee (ICC).
